1. Motivating example

1. Motivating example

  1. Research question: “To what extent do different teaching methods influence students’ ability to produce high-quality written texts?”
  2. Method: Randomization and a CJ experiment, specifically:
  • Researchers randomly assign students to two different teaching method
  • Students write one or more texts
  • Researchers collect students’ written texts
  • Researchers randomly assign judges to compare pairs of students’ written texts
  • Judges produce a dichotomous outcome, reflecting the relative quality of each text

  1. Estimand: “On average, is there a difference in the ability to produce high-quality written texts between the two groups of students?”

  2. Estimator: the BTL model

  3. Estimates: estimated BTL scores, or their transformations

3. Some issues with the theory

It is intuitive to think that:

The outcome’s ability to reflect the “true” differences between texts largely depends on the validity of the model’s assumptions [1].

So, what are the issues:

  1. Excessive reliance on Case V’s assumptions for the analysis of CJ data

    \(\longrightarrow\) the assumption of equal dispersion, and zero correlation between stimuli

  2. Disconnect between trait measurement and hypothesis testing

3.1 Case V and statistical analysis

3.1 Case V and statistical analysis

But, are Case V assumptions violated?

  1. Thurstone: equal dispersion may not be applicable when researchers assess complex traits or heterogeneous stimuli, like texts!

    \(\longrightarrow\) Evidence of this can be found in misfit statistics?

  2. Zero correlation is almost certainly invalid in two scenarios:

    \(\longrightarrow\) when intricate aspects of texts generate judges’ biases, ultimately introducing dependencies between texts.

    \(\longrightarrow\) when an additional hierarchical structure is relevant to the texts.

Equal dispersion:

  • “misfits” texts are texts with more judgment discrepancies than others [2–5]
  • “misfit” judges are judges who deviate substantially from the shared consensus.

Zero correlation:

  • additional relevant characteristics, exert an uneven influence on judges’ perceptions, creating biases. Since the discriminal difference of the stimuli becomes an observable outcome only through the judges’ perceptions, these biases introduce dependencies between the stimuli.
  • the shared context created by additional hierarchical structures introduce dependencies between stimuli (clustering) Not properly handled

3.1 Case V and statistical analysis

But, what are the problems with this?

  1. Different dispersions, neglecting judges’ biases and hierarchical structures

    \(\longrightarrow\) reduce precision of parameter estimates

    \(\longrightarrow\) overestimation of trait’s reliability

    \(\longrightarrow\) drawing spurious conclusions [6]

  2. Conventional practice of discarding ‘misfit’ observations [2–5]

    \(\longrightarrow\) discard valuable information

    \(\longrightarrow\) introduce bias into trait estimates [6, 7]

    \(\longrightarrow\) drawing spurious conclusions [6]

3.2 Measurement and hypothesis tests

3.2 Measurement and hypothesis tests

Researchers use the BTL model as a measurement model for CJ [3–5, 8–12], i.e., they use the estimated BTL scores, or their transformations, to conduct additional analyses or hypothesis tests, for example:

  • Identify ‘misfit’ judges and stimuli [3–5]
  • Detect biases in judges’ ratings [3, 13]
  • Calculate correlations with other assessment methods [5, 12],
  • Test hypotheses related to the underlying trait of interest [12, 14–19]

3.2 Measurement and hypothesis tests

But, what are the problems with this?

  1. Ignoring uncertainty of BTL scores

    \(\longrightarrow\) introduce bias + reduce the precision of hypothesis tests [20, 21]

    \(\longrightarrow\) diminish statistical power of tests

    \(\longrightarrow\) increase likelihood of type-I or type-II errors [6], i.e., drawing spurious conclusions.

  • We need a more systematic and integrated framework that enhances statistical accuracy while strengthening measurement reliability and validity.

6. Discussion

6.1 Discussion: Findings

We have proposed a model that:

  1. Shifts the analysis paradigm from passively accepting to actively testing whether the assumptions fit the data [26]. Specifically, we have lifted the necessity of assuming:
  1. Zero correlation between texts
  2. Separate process for trait measurement and hypothesis testing
  3. Equal dispersions between students or texts
  4. No judgment biases (new)
  5. Dichotomous outcome (new)
  6. Need for removal of ‘misfit’ observations (new)
  7. Normal \(e_{IA}, e_{I}, e_{JK},e_{J}\) idiosyncrasies (new)\(^{1}\)
  8. Only two hierarchical components (new)\(^{1}\)
  9. Multidimensional traits (new)\(^{1}\)
  1. Stretching a bit the possibilities. Requires simulation analysis.

6.1 Discussion: Findings

We have proposed a model that:

  1. Clarify interactions among actors involved in CJ experiments [26]:
  1. Students and texts
  2. Judges and judgments (new)
  1. Clarify interactions among processes involved in CJ experiments [26]:

  1. The sample mechanism is a generator of missing data,

    \(\longrightarrow\) with the sample size of students, text, and judges, and how to work with random and non-random samples (new)

  2. The comparison mechanism is a generator of missing data,

    \(\longrightarrow\) with the number of judgments per judge, do repeated judgments bring benefits?, is there a benefit in adaptive comparative algorithms? (new)

6.2 Discussion: Limitations

  1. Not tested yet? (context dependent)

  2. Researcher need a deep knowledge of Causal inference

    \(\longrightarrow\) e.g., what does it mean for predictor variables \(X_{IA}, X_{I}, Z_{JK}, Z_{J}\) to be part of the sufficient adjustment set.

  3. Researcher need knowledge of Probabilistic programming languages

  4. Researcher need knowledge of Bayesian inference methods

a sufficient adjustment set of variables (potentially an empty set) refers to those that satisfy the backdoor criterion [25], that is, variables that block all non-causal paths without introducing new ones [22, 23, 27].

Naturally, the validity of causal claims now hinges on the assumption that these variables serve as a sufficient adjustment set, a property that cannot be guaranteed for any variable. Nevertheless, as [1] noted, drawing conclusions about the real world from observed data inevitably requires assumptions, and these requirements hold true for both observational and experimental data.

6.2 Discussion: Further research

  1. Model testing trough simulation and power analysis (context dependent)
  2. Replication studies:
  1. “New” rules of thumb for sample sizes
  2. “New” rules of thumb for number of comparisons
  3. Are there benefits of adaptive comparative algorithms?
  4. Effects of predictor variables on students, texts, judges, and judgments
  5. Are additive better than multiplicative judges’ biases (akin to discrimination parameter in IRT)
  6. etc. etc. etc.
